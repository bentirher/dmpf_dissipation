# Open-System Generalization of Middle-Out Contraction (MOC) — Working Summary

**Purpose of this document:** consolidated state of the investigation into whether the middle-out
contraction (MOC) algorithm for dynamic multi-product formulas (MPF) can be efficiently generalized
from closed (unitary) to open (Lindbladian) quantum dynamics. Written so that a new chat can pick up
research directly from here without re-deriving settled results. References to "the draft" mean
`main.pdf`; "the closed-system paper" means `tensor_network_enhanced_dynamic_MPF.pdf` (Robertson et
al.); "the Liouville review" means `2003_11472v3.pdf` (Gyamfi).

---

## 1. Background and setup

### 1.1 Closed-system baseline (Robertson et al.)

For Hamiltonian dynamics, the dynamic MPF approximates $\rho(t) \approx \mu(t) = \sum_j c_j(t)\rho_{k_j}(t)$,
where $\rho_{k_j}(t)$ is generated by a $k_j$-step Trotter circuit. Coefficients $c_j(t)$ minimize the
Frobenius-norm Trotter error, which reduces to a linear system built from:
- $M_{ij}(t) = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{k_i}(t)\rho_{k_j}(t))$
- $L_j(t) = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho(t)\rho_{k_j}(t))$

For **pure-state, unitary** evolution this collapses to $M_{ij} = |\langle\psi_0|F_{ij}|\psi_0\rangle|^2$
with $F_{ij} \equiv U_i^\dagger U_j$, where $U_i = S(t/k_i)^{k_i}$. The **MOC efficiency claim** rests
entirely on: $U^\dagger = U^{-1}$ for unitary $U$, so $F_{ii} = \mathbb{1}$ **exactly**, and $F_{i\ne j}$
stays close to identity for similar $k_i,k_j$. This lets Algorithm 1 build $F_{ij}$ incrementally
(alternating forward $S$ and adjoint $S^\dagger$ layers, SVD-truncating at each step) while bond
dimension stays small — because the operator never drifts far from identity.

**This is the single mechanism the whole closed-system efficiency argument rests on. Everything in
this project is about whether an analogue of it survives for open systems.**

### 1.2 Open-system generalization (the draft, Section IV)

Vectorize $\rho \to |\rho\rangle\rangle$ (column-stacking convention: $\mathrm{vec}(AXB) = (B^T\otimes A)\mathrm{vec}(X)$).
The Lindbladian becomes a superoperator $\mathcal{L}$ on the $4^n$-dimensional Liouville space:
$$\mathcal{L}[\rho] = -i[H,\rho] + \sum_\alpha \gamma_\alpha\Big(L_\alpha\rho L_\alpha^\dagger - \tfrac12\{L_\alpha^\dagger L_\alpha,\rho\}\Big)$$
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{vec}} = -i(\mathbb{1}\otimes H - H^T\otimes\mathbb{1}) + \sum_\alpha\gamma_\alpha\Big(L_\alpha^*\otimes L_\alpha - \tfrac12\mathbb{1}\otimes L_\alpha^\dagger L_\alpha - \tfrac12(L_\alpha^\dagger L_\alpha)^T\otimes\mathbb{1}\Big)$$
$S(\Delta t) \equiv e^{\Delta t\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{vec}}}$ is the one-step channel as a $4^n\times4^n$ matrix.

**Key structural fact (Gyamfi review):** $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vec}}$ is, for $\gamma>0$, neither Hermitian
nor skew-Hermitian. This is the root cause of everything that follows — there is no orthogonal
eigenbasis to exploit, and crucially **$S^\dagger \ne S^{-1}$** once dissipation is present (they
coincide only for unitary $S$).

## 2. Settled derivation: $F_{ij}$ must use the adjoint, not the inverse

This was a genuine point of confusion early on (the draft's Eq. 50–51 inherits ambiguous notation
from the closed-system paper) but is now **fully resolved, derived from first principles, and
numerically verified**:

$$M_{ij}(t) = \langle\langle\rho_{k_i}(t)|\rho_{k_j}(t)\rangle\rangle = \langle\langle\rho(0)|\,\big(S(t/k_i)^{k_i}\big)^\dagger\,S(t/k_j)^{k_j}\,|\rho(0)\rangle\rangle$$

Pulling an operator from ket to bra side of an inner product **always** produces the adjoint, never
the inverse — this is forced by the definition of inner product, not a modeling choice. Numerically
verified: using $S^\dagger$ reproduces the brute-force $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{k_i}\rho_{k_j})$ exactly;
using $S^{-1}$ does not (gives a substantially wrong number).

**Action item for the draft:** Eq. 50–51 and the surrounding prose need correcting. The current text
says "$S^\dagger$ (adjoint)... is not necessarily a physical backward evolution because the inverse of
a channel need not be physical" — this conflates adjoint and inverse. The *adjoint* $S^\dagger$ is
itself always well-defined and (we showed) is **positive and unital** (never violates positivity,
unlike the inverse) but is **not trace-preserving**. The correct statement: the near-cancellation
$S^\dagger S\approx\mathbb{1}$ that holds for unitary $S$ fails for genuine CPTP $S$, because adjoint
and inverse coincide only in the unitary case.

### Reference for this fact (found via literature search, useful for the writeup):
- Standard Stinespring duality: a linear map is trace-preserving **iff** its Hilbert-Schmidt dual is
  unital (cited in MAT4430 lecture notes, Univ. Oslo, and standard quantum channel texts).
- **No-go theorem** (directly relevant, found via search — *"Simulation of Adjoints and Petz Recovery
  Maps for Unknown Quantum Channels"*, arXiv:2602.05828): the adjoint of a general quantum channel
  **cannot be implemented by any completely positive supermap, even probabilistically** (only the
  transpose can be, via post-selected teleportation). This is the rigorous statement behind why no
  Stinespring/unitary-dilation trick can ever produce $S^\dagger$ as an honest physical process — see
  Section 5 below.

## 3. Numerical results: vectorized/Liouville-space route (dense, no TN yet)

### 3.1 Sign-convention bug (now fixed, does not affect any reported scalar)

Early in this work, `sigma_minus` was defined as $\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$, which is
**backwards** — that matrix is the *raising* operator $|1\rangle\langle0|$, not the lowering operator.
Correct amplitude-damping operator (with $|0\rangle$=ground, $|1\rangle$=excited):
$$L = \sigma_- = |0\rangle\langle1| = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$$
**This bug was caught and fixed.** Because raising/lowering are related by relabeling $|0\rangle\leftrightarrow|1\rangle$
(a basis permutation), **all Frobenius norms and scalar results computed before the fix are numerically
identical after the fix** — verified explicitly. Only the *physical narrative* attached to specific
matrix entries (which state is the dissipative fixed point) was backwards in early discussion. If
continuing this work, **use the corrected operator above** and double check any new physical-language
claims (e.g. "population flows toward $|0\rangle$") against it.

### 3.2 Analytical result: $F_{ii} = S^\dagger(t)S(t)$ for single-qubit dissipators

**Exact, closed-form result for amplitude damping** ($L=\sigma_-$, any $H\propto Z$ — the result is
exactly independent of the Hamiltonian frequency $\omega$):
$$\|F_{ii}-\mathbb{1}\|_F = 2\sqrt{e^{2\gamma t}+1}\;e^{-2\gamma t}\,\big|e^{\gamma t}-1\big|$$
Low-damping expansion: $\|F_{ii}-\mathbb{1}\|_F \approx 2\sqrt2\,\gamma t + O((\gamma t)^2)$ —
**confirms smooth, continuous recovery of the closed-system result ($F_{ii}=\mathbb{1}$) as $\gamma\to0$.**

**General single-qubit result** (any Lindblad operator $L = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}$,
leading order in $\gamma\Delta t$, Hamiltonian part drops out at this order):
$$\|F_{ii}-\mathbb{1}\|_F \approx \gamma\Delta t\,\big\|\mathcal{L}_1+\mathcal{L}_1^\dagger\big\|_F + O((\gamma\Delta t)^2)$$
where $\mathcal{L}_1 = L^*\otimes L - \tfrac12\mathbb{1}\otimes L^\dagger L - \tfrac12(L^\dagger L)^T\otimes\mathbb{1}$
is the dissipative part of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vec}}$ per unit $\gamma$. Numerically verified prefactors:

| Channel | $L$ | $\|\mathcal{L}_1+\mathcal{L}_1^\dagger\|_F$ |
|---|---|---|
| Amplitude damping | $\sigma_-$ | $2\sqrt2 \approx 2.83$ |
| Dephasing | $\sigma_z$ | $4\sqrt2 \approx 5.66$ |
| Bit-flip ($\sigma_x$, one Pauli component of depolarizing) | $\sigma_x$ | $4\sqrt2 \approx 5.66$ |

**Headline structural finding:** regardless of which single-qubit dissipator, the breakdown of the
diagonal near-identity property is **always exactly linear in $\gamma\Delta t$** to leading order —
channel-independent in form, only the prefactor varies (~2x between these three channels).

**Caveat:** this is a single-layer, single-site, diagonal-only result. It does NOT by itself predict
multi-layer/multi-site bond-dimension growth (see 3.3).

### 3.3 Numerical result: bond-dimension/Schmidt-rank growth (the real finding)

Built small chains (4–6 qubits, Heisenberg $H$, one local amplitude-damping dissipator per site),
constructed the running $F_{ii} = (S^\dagger)^kS^k$ object layer-by-layer (dense, no TN — values
exact, exhaustively verified, but limited to small systems and few layers by memory/CPU), and
measured the operator-Schmidt rank across a middle bipartition (reshaping the $4^n\times4^n$ matrix
as input/output legs grouped left vs. right, $d=4$ per site since each site carries both an "in" and
"out" Liouville index).

**Closed system ($\gamma=0$):** rank = exactly 1, forever, at every layer. $\|F_{ii}-\mathbb{1}\|_F=0$
exactly. Matches the closed-system paper's claim trivially (exact cancellation, no truncation needed).

**Open system, any $\gamma>0$:** rank grows **fast and accelerates toward the full available dimension**
within a handful of layers (e.g. $n=4$: rank 45→76→94→146→188→208 over 6 layers at $\gamma=0.02$,
already at 122→185→243→254→256→256 by layer 6 at $\gamma=0.1$ — saturating the $256=4^4$ ceiling).
Confirmed not a small-system artifact: same fast, accelerating growth seen at $n=5,6$ (limited data at
$n=6$ due to compute cost, but rank 148→436 between layers 1–2 out of a ceiling of 4096 — still
clearly accelerating, nowhere near the ceiling, same qualitative shape).

**Interpretation (important, and somewhat counterintuitive — resolved):** this does NOT mean
dissipation increases the entanglement of the *physical state* $\rho(t)$ (it typically does the
opposite — drives toward a low-entropy fixed point). $F_{ii}$ is a purely formal bookkeeping operator
on the Liouville space, never applied to an actual physical state in the algorithm. Its blow-up
reflects that composing many non-unitary maps and their adjoints behaves like **generic operator
composition** (no special structure), which generically builds up entanglement/operator complexity —
in sharp contrast to the closed case where $U^\dagger U=\mathbb{1}$ is an *exact algebraic identity*,
not a statement about physical complexity. **The "miracle" behind MOC's efficiency was unitarity-specific
algebra, not a generic property of "evolution operators are simple."** Once unitarity is dropped, even
slightly, the generic (unhelpful, fast-growing) behavior reasserts itself.

**Conclusion so far: the direct vectorized/Liouville-space route to $F_{ij}$ does not look tractable
as an MPO — bond dimension appears to race toward the maximum, not stay controlled.**

## 4. Stinespring dilation route — purified-state simulation

### 4.1 Motivation

Idea (user's): embed system+environment in a larger Hilbert space where the *joint* evolution is
exactly unitary (Stinespring dilation), restoring $U_{SE}^\dagger U_{SE}=\mathbb{1}$ exactly. Concrete
circuit for amplitude damping, **verified correct** against the Lindblad-equation solution:
- Qubits ordered (system, ancilla), ancilla starts in $|0\rangle$.
- $\sin^2(\theta/2) = 1-e^{-\gamma\Delta t}$.
- Gate 1: $CR_y(\theta)$, **control = system, target = ancilla**.
- Gate 2: $CX$, **control = ancilla, target = system**.
- (Order matters and was a real debugging exercise — the reverse CX assignment gives a different,
  wrong channel. Verified by checking the known transformation
  $\alpha|0\rangle_s|0\rangle_e+\beta|1\rangle_s|0\rangle_e \to \alpha|0\rangle_s|0\rangle_e + \beta\cos\theta|1\rangle_s|0\rangle_e+\beta\sin\theta|0\rangle_s|1\rangle_e$
  against several open-systems-on-quantum-hardware papers, e.g. arXiv:1906.07099, arXiv:1404.3747.)

### 4.2 Numerical result: purified-state entanglement grows slowly and plateaus

Built chains of system qubits (Heisenberg $H$) each coupled to a **fresh** ancilla per Trotter step
(no reset — full unitary history kept, since reset is itself the irreversible step and resetting would
trivially hide the quantity being measured). Tracked Schmidt rank of the full purified state
(system + all ancillas used so far) across a middle cut.

**Result:** slow, clearly **decelerating** growth, plateauing at a modest value well below the
theoretical maximum (e.g. $n_{\text{sites}}=3$: ranks 5→6→7→7→7, plateauing at 7, vs. a theoretical
ceiling that grows 8→16→64→128→512 over the same layers; $n_{\text{sites}}=4$ still climbing but
decelerating — 10→14→16→19→21 over 5 layers, increments shrinking). **Qualitatively opposite behavior
to the vectorized $F_{ii}$ route (Section 3.3).** Makes physical sense: this is a genuine physical
state under local unitary dynamics, which is exactly the regime tensor networks are built to exploit
(area-law-like entanglement growth), unlike the artificial, structureless $F_{ii}$ bookkeeping object.

**Important resource caveat:** this requires $k$ ancilla qubits for $k$ Trotter steps if you need the
full *lossless* unitary history (e.g. to later try a cancellation trick). Reset-and-reuse (1 ancilla
per system qubit, reused with reset each step) is how you'd actually implement/simulate the *forward*
channel efficiently — but reset is the very non-unitary step whose effect we were trying to quantify,
so it can't be used when testing whether a cancellation mechanism exists.

### 4.3 Critical limitation — established as a hard no-go, not just "not yet found"

**This is the most important negative result of the whole investigation. Read carefully before
re-attempting dilation-based cancellation tricks.**

Computing $M_{ij}=\mathrm{Tr}_S(\rho_{k_i}\rho_{k_j})$ from two dilated/purified trajectories
fundamentally requires either:
(a) **two independent copies** of system+bath and a SWAP-test-style overlap (mathematically: embed
both reduced states' purifications and contract $\mathrm{Tr}(\mathrm{SWAP}\cdot\rho_i\otimes\rho_j)$ —
note: for a *classical* TN computation we don't need a literal SWAP-test circuit, just the equivalent
MPO-MPO trace contraction of the two reduced density operators); or
(b) a genuine forward/backward sandwich $\mathcal{U}_i^\dagger\mathcal{U}_j$ analogous to the closed
case — but this requires a physical realization of $S^\dagger$ as a dilatable unitary process, which
provably **does not exist**:

> $S^\dagger$ is unital but not trace-preserving (verified numerically: positivity is preserved,
> trace is not). By Stinespring duality, only trace-preserving maps have the
> $\mathrm{Tr}_E[U(\cdot\otimes|0\rangle\langle0|)U^\dagger]$ representation. Confirmed further by an
> explicit no-go theorem (arXiv:2602.05828): **the adjoint of an unknown/general quantum channel
> cannot be implemented by any completely positive map, even probabilistically** (only the transpose
> can be, via post-selected teleportation).

**Conclusion: route (b) — recovering an honest MOC-style forward/backward cancellation via Stinespring
dilation — is closed off on principled mathematical grounds, not merely "hard."** Route (a) is viable
as a *way to compute $M_{ij}$ at all* and benefits from the favorable entanglement-growth numerics in
4.2, but it is **operationally equivalent to "simulate the trajectory and store the state"** — it does
NOT inherit or replicate MOC's specific efficiency mechanism (avoiding ever building the state via an
exact operator-level cancellation). It may still be a useful, better-than-baseline way to simulate
open dynamics with MPS methods, but it should not be oversold as "open-system MOC."

### 4.4 Role of Kraus operators (side question, resolved)

Kraus form ($S(\rho)=\sum_\mu K_\mu\rho K_\mu^\dagger$, $\sum_\mu K_\mu^\dagger K_\mu=\mathbb{1}$) is
the natural bridge between channel and dilation (Stinespring unitary is built by stacking Kraus
operators), and is likely the most economical MPO representation of the **forward** channel $S$ itself
(worth benchmarking in the implementation chat). It does **not** resolve the adjoint problem: in Kraus
language, $S^\dagger(B)=\sum_\mu K_\mu^\dagger BK_\mu$, and the completeness relation that would make
this trace-preserving is $\sum_\mu K_\mu K_\mu^\dagger=\mathbb{1}$ — generally a *different* condition
than $\sum_\mu K_\mu^\dagger K_\mu=\mathbb{1}$ (they coincide only for normal/unitary $K_\mu$). So Kraus
form makes the obstruction explicit and computable, but doesn't remove it.

## 5. Promising open lead: preserved sectors for dephasing-type dissipators

**This is the most promising unresolved direction — the recommended starting point for further
analytical/numerical work, distinct from (and not subject to) the no-go result in 4.3, since it works
entirely within the vectorized/Liouville picture without needing any dilation.**

### 5.1 The result

For **any** Lindblad operator diagonal in the computational basis, $L=\mathrm{diag}(a,b)$ with real
$a,b$ (e.g. dephasing, $L=\sigma_z\Rightarrow a=1,b=-1$), and **any** Hamiltonian (tested with $H=0$;
generalizes whenever $[H,L]=0$, i.e. $H$ diagonal in the same basis):

$$F_{ii} = S^\dagger(t)S(t) = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0&0\\0&e^{-\gamma t(a-b)^2}&0&0\\0&0&e^{-\gamma t(a-b)^2}&0\\0&0&0&1\end{pmatrix}$$

**The population/diagonal sector (entries 1 and 4, i.e. $\rho_{00},\rho_{11}$) is EXACTLY $\mathbb{1}$
— algebraically exact, for any $\gamma$, $t$, no approximation, no small-parameter expansion needed.**
All the departure from identity is confined to the **coherence sector** (entries 2,3 — $\rho_{01},\rho_{10}$),
with an exact, simple closed-form decay rate $e^{-\gamma t(a-b)^2}$.

**Mechanism (why this happens, stated so it generalizes correctly):** a dephasing-type dissipator
commutes with the populations it's diagonal in — $\dot\rho_{00}=\dot\rho_{11}=0$ from the dissipator
alone, at the master-equation level, not just for $F_{ii}$. If $H$ also doesn't move population
between those basis states (e.g. $[H,L]=0$), the **entire population subspace evolves unitarily**
even though the full system is genuinely dissipative elsewhere. MOC-style exact cancellation survives
intact on this subspace for free.

### 5.2 What's NOT yet checked (next steps for this lead)

1. **Does amplitude damping have NO analogous preserved sector?** Prediction (not yet verified): damping
   moves population between $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$, so there should be no basis in which it acts
   trivially on a nontrivial subspace — making dephasing-type noise structurally privileged over
   damping-type noise for this approach. This is a clean, checkable claim (symbolic, like Section 3.2's
   calculations) that should be verified before relying on it.
2. **Does the preserved-sector property survive at the level of bond dimension in the many-site,
   many-layer chain numerics (Section 3.3)?** I.e., if you restrict the MOC computation to
   population-sector observables/overlaps only (discard or never compute coherence-sector pieces),
   does the bond-dimension blow-up from Section 3.3 get tamed? This has NOT been tested numerically yet
   — natural next step, reusing the chain_lib.py infrastructure from Section 3.3 but with
   dephasing-type dissipators and a rank measurement restricted to the relevant sector.
3. **Off-diagonal case ($k_i\ne k_j$):** all of Section 5.1 is for $F_{ii}$ only. Whether/how the
   preserved-sector property extends to $F_{i\ne j}$ (different Trotter step counts) is unchecked.
4. **Multi-site generalization:** checked only for single-site dephasing. For a chain with one
   dephasing dissipator per site, does the *joint* population sector (product of all sites' populations)
   remain exactly preserved, or do cross-site Hamiltonian terms spoil this once $H$ has two-site
   coupling that doesn't commute with the global dephasing structure? This is likely the single most
   important thing to check next, since the single-qubit result alone doesn't establish anything about
   scaling to a many-body chain — and the original closed-system efficiency claim is explicitly a
   many-body one.
5. **Practical scope:** even if confirmed, this gives a "partial MOC" — efficient only for observables/
   overlaps expressible within the preserved population sector (e.g. $\langle\sigma_z\rangle$-type
   correlators), not the full Trotter-error/MPF machinery. Worth thinking about whether this is a
   useful enough restriction to build a real result around, or whether it should be framed as "this is
   the boundary of what's achievable, here is exactly where it breaks."

## 6. Summary table — status of each route

| Route | Recovers MOC's exact cancellation mechanism? | Bond-dim/Schmidt-rank behavior found | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct vectorized Liouville-space ($F_{ij}$ via $S^\dagger$, no dilation) | No — $S^\dagger S\ne\mathbb{1}$, proven algebraically and numerically | Fast, accelerating growth toward max in small-chain tests | Looks intractable as-is |
| Stinespring dilation, independent-copy/SWAP route | No — operationally equivalent to trajectory simulation, no operator-level cancellation | Slow, plateauing growth (favorable!) | Useful as *better simulator*, not as MOC-replacement |
| Stinespring dilation, shared-ancilla cancellation attempt | **No — closed off by no-go theorem** ($S^\dagger$ not CP-realizable) | N/A — construction doesn't exist | Dead end, principled |
| Kraus formalism | N/A (representation tool, not a new mechanism) | Not yet benchmarked | Useful for forward-$S$ MPO efficiency; doesn't fix adjoint problem |
| Dephasing-type preserved-sector restriction | **Yes, exactly, on a restricted sector** | Not yet tested at chain/multi-layer level | **Most promising open lead** |
